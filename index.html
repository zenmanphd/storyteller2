Chapter 3 - Meeting Agent Rutger



My head was spinning after leaving Hopkins at the fire station that afternoon. The time stamped video footage of lightning syncing up to the date and time of each disappearance felt like a discovery, like holding a missing piece, but to what puzzle?
Pieces were fitting together somehow but they weren’t forming any picture, and yet I felt as though I was in fact learning something. What did the plant have to do with it; nothing or everything? Aside from a few freaky lights caught on poorly pixelated camera images. I had nothing substantial to go banging on their doors about. Of course, who’s to say they won’t reach out to me first.
Of course, I can’t say my Fire tower visit was completely wasted. That map of Teddy’s along with the clever markings she’d made tickled my curiosity a great deal. There was one point on the map she’d overlooked, a missing person she wouldn’t have known about. His case wasn’t mentioned in the news with the others. He wasn’t even a hiker, and yet all the other points seemed to form a jagged circle around the spot where he went missing, Arnold’s Pier.
Hard to say what I was doing out there that next morning. I couldn’t sleep and Miskwà was only a 7-minute drive. “Couldn’t sleep” is putting it lightly. For weeks it felt like all my nightmares had been written and directed by the Devil himself. One dream kept leading me to the same place in the same moment.
I’m running down the boardwalk of an old pier. My lungs are burning in the cold, and I don’t know what I’m trying to catch up to, but I reach the water and there he is, Malcolm Reese (the 1st missing person). He’s being suspended in the air just 3 feet above the water’s surface like a G.I. Joe action figure. He sees me but can’t move, mouth wide open trying to scream, but no voice to carry it.
And I can’t move. Something is holding me back; powerful, ancient, and emotionless. And it’s coming from the Goddamn water! I know it is, but I don’t know how I know. Sure, his file says he’s 22 yrs. old but his eyes are 5 yrs. old, still afraid of the dark.
As I fought against my invisible shackles I watched helplessly as an empty sphere of light sucked him into an unknown hell one atom at a time.
So, armed with a McMuffin, my service pistol, and my day off sweatpants I wandered through the morning dew to Arnold’s pier that day. It had been 3 months since Malcolm disappeared and I’m revisiting a cold scene with no idea what I’m looking for.
Malcolm’s case was fundamentally different than the others. While he may have disappeared by the water like the others, it is well known that his behavior changed drastically in the hours leading up to his disappearance.
I knew him. He’s gone for a ride in my squad car more than once for shoplifting and petty theft while he was in Highschool. We were all glad to see him get his shit together eventually, securing an apprenticeship with an electrician through Carver Community College.

Ten minutes later there was a resounding boom heard throughout the building. Bishop and the front desk manager snatched an AED from the wall and hauled ass to the roof as fast as his 52 yr. old knees could get him there. Sure enough, Malcolm was sprawled out by an air conditioning duct blinking hard, eyes to the horizon. He was dazed, still gripping a pair of needle nose pliers with his gloved right hand. He had no clue what happened, only that it got so eerily cloudy at one point that he reached for the flashlight on his keychain. Before he knew it, he was on the ground. As they got him to his feet the concrete was scorched underneath him. His steel toe work boots were kicked off to the side, soaking wet.
They got Malcolm into the passenger seat of Bishop’s truck where he waited while Bishop made one last sweep of the roof for live wires or faulty connections, which he never found. When he returned, Malcolm was gone.
Two loggers were tying down their cargo along the old service road about a quarter mile away. They called the park ranger when they spotted a “tall, young guy in gray coveralls walking barefoot down the gravel path talking to himself.” He was headed in the direction of Arnold’s pier. That would be the last time anyone saw Malcolm.

Now there I was, standing at the end of the pier. A flock of waterfowl took flight suddenly from the surface of the water, an area that looked much deeper or at least darker. It formed a barely perceivable ring of black water that seemed to churn counter to the flow of the stream that fed it. My eyes fixed on that point in the distance. Perhaps the water was churning about the branches of a fallen, submerged tree or the buried, abandoned architecture of long departed beavers. However, the overall shape was far too defined to be caused by something natural. Leaving the pier, I moved along the bank to get a better look.
